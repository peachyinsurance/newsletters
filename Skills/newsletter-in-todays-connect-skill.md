# In Today's Connect Writer

Writes the "In Today's Connect" teaser section for East Cobb Connect (and Perimeter Post) newsletter editions. This section sits between the editor's note and the first content block. Its job is to build curiosity and get readers scrolling, not to summarize what's inside.

---

## What This Section Is (and Isn't)

**Is:** A set of short, curiosity-driven teaser lines that preview the most interesting content in the edition. Each line should make a reader want to scroll down and find it.

**Is not:**
- A table of contents (the newsletter has a separate TOC with section names only)
- A summary of the edition
- A labeled index (no "Featured event:", "Restaurant pick:", "Local news:" prefixes)

The distinction matters. A TOC tells you where things are. This section tells you *why you should care*.

---

## Hard Rules

1. **No section labels.** Never prefix a line with the section it belongs to. No "Featured event:", "Restaurant pick:", "Business brief:", "Real estate:", "Local Lowdown:", or any variation. The reader doesn't need to know which section something lives in — they need a reason to scroll.

2. **One emoji per line, leading.** Each line starts with a single emoji that fits the content. The emoji is the visual bullet point. No trailing emojis, no double emojis.

3. **No em dashes.** Use commas or natural sentence structure instead. Em dashes create visual clutter at this line length.

4. **No line wrapping.** Every line must fit on a single line in a standard email client. Target roughly 55–65 characters max per line (including the emoji). If it wraps, it's too long. Cut it.

5. **5–8 lines total.** Enough to represent the edition's range without becoming a wall of text. 7 is the sweet spot. Pull from across the edition's content — events, restaurants, business, news, real estate, pets — but not every section needs a line.

6. **Every item must be real and in the edition.** No teasing content that doesn't appear in the newsletter. No invented hooks.

7. **No hype language.** No "Don't miss," "You won't want to miss," "Must-see," or "Can't-miss." Be concrete and let the content sell itself.

8. **No exclamation marks.** Keep the energy confident and casual, not breathless.

9. **Sort by interest, not by section order.** Lead with the strongest hook. End with something warm or light (pet adoption works well as a closer). The middle should alternate between types to create variety.

## Writing Style

> **Voice:** Tone, rhythm, and style come from the house voice guide provided above this skill at generation time. Apply it. The rules below are this section's specifics: format, length, structure, selection, and output.

**Line structure patterns that work:**

| Pattern | Example |
|---|---|
| The [specific thing] you [relatable action] | The hand-pulled noodle spot on Johnson Ferry you keep driving past |
| [Noun] at [Place] [Day], [bonus detail] | Kathleen Madigan at Cobb Energy Saturday, no downtown traffic |
| [Number] + [thing] in [location/range] | Three homes in 30062 from $295K to $895K |
| [Subject] just [verb] [accomplishment] | Two Wheeler robotics teams just punched their ticket to state |
| Meet [Name], [single compelling detail] | Meet Mocha, couch-ready and waiting since November |
| [Place]'s [thing] [verb] [day] at [time] | Shaw Park's redevelopment plans go public Thursday at 6 PM |
| A [number] sq ft [thing] with [standout feature] | A 7,000 sq ft indoor playground with no time limits |

## How to Select Items

Read the full edition first. Then pick 5–8 items using this priority:

1. **The featured event or biggest weekend draw** — almost always gets a line
2. **The restaurant or food pick** — high curiosity potential, readers love food hooks
3. **The strongest news/civic item** — especially if time-sensitive (a meeting, a vote, a deadline)
4. **A surprising or unusual item** — the thing readers wouldn't expect (robotics team, a niche business, a free activity)
5. **Real estate** — if there's a good range or an open house, one line can cover all listings
6. **The adoptable pet** — works as a warm closer almost every time
7. **A business brief** — only if it has a genuinely interesting hook (not just "new business opens")
8. **A secondary event or free activity** — if it adds variety or fills a day gap

**Skip items that:**
- Don't have an interesting angle at this length
- Would require too much context to make sense in one line
- Duplicate the energy of another line (two food lines, two event lines)

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Fails | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| "🌿 **Featured event:** Garden Storytime at East Cobb Library" | Section label kills the curiosity — it reads like a TOC entry | "🌿 A garden-themed storytime at East Cobb Library Friday morning" |
| "🍝 **Restaurant pick:** La Strada (East Cobb)" | Label + name + location with zero hook | "🍝 The Italian spot on Johnson Ferry with $14 handmade pasta" |
| "🏡 **Real estate picks:** starter, sweet spot, and showcase" | Jargon labels, no concrete detail | "🏡 Three homes in 30062 from $295K to $895K" |
| "📰 **Local Lowdown:** 5 East Cobb stories from the past week" | Generic summary, no reason to scroll | Pick the single most interesting story and tease that |
| Lines that run 80+ characters | Wraps in email, looks cluttered, loses the clean list feel | Cut to ~60 characters. If you can't, you haven't found the hook yet |
| Using em dashes to connect clauses | Visual clutter at this line length | Use commas or restructure the sentence |
| Ending with a news/civic item | Ends the section on a heavy note | Close with the pet adoption or a light/fun item |

## Output Format

Always deliver as a single block with the header and emoji-led lines:

```
**In Today's Connect:**

🎤 [teaser line]

🥟 [teaser line]

🤖 [teaser line]

🛝 [teaser line]

🌳 [teaser line]

🏡 [teaser line]

🐾 [teaser line]
```

Each line is separated by a blank line for readability in email. Bold the "In Today's Connect:" header only.

---

## Inputs Needed Before Writing

If not already provided in context, you need:

1. The full edition content (or at minimum, the key items from each section)
2. Which item the editor considers the lead/strongest hook

If you have the full edition, select and write without asking. If you only have a partial list, ask what's missing before writing.
