# Newsletter Headline Writer

Writes the subject line and preview text pair for East Cobb Connect and Perimeter Post newsletter editions. These two elements are the single biggest lever on open rate. They work as a unit: the subject hooks, the preview expands.

---

## The Job

The reader sees three things before deciding to open:

1. **Sender name** — East Cobb Connect or Perimeter Post (already locked in)
2. **Subject line** — the headline (30–45 characters visible on mobile)
3. **Preview text** — the subhead (40–90 characters visible depending on device/client)

The subject must make the reader curious or create recognition. The preview must reward that curiosity with new information. They must never say the same thing twice.

---

## Hard Rules

1. **Subject line: 30–45 characters.** Under 40 is ideal. Over 50 is a failure. Count every character including spaces and emoji.
2. **Preview text: 60–110 characters.** Front-load the most important words. Some clients cut after 40 characters.
3. **One emoji max in the subject.** Zero is also fine. Never more than one. Emoji counts as ~2 characters.
4. **No category words as leads.** Never open with: "Guide," "Planner," "Picks," "Tips," "Roundup," "Digest," "Update," or "More." These are inbox wallpaper.
5. **No "Your Weekend" or "This Week" as the first words.** Every local newsletter says this. It's invisible.
6. **Preview text must add new hooks.** If the subject mentions Kathleen Madigan, the preview should NOT mention Kathleen Madigan. It should mention Wheeler Robotics, or a restaurant, or Shaw Park.
7. **Every item named must be real and in the edition.** No invented hooks. No promises the issue doesn't deliver on.
8. **No ALL CAPS in the subject line.** Ever.
9. **No clickbait.** "You won't BELIEVE what opened on Johnson Ferry" is banned. Be concrete, not manipulative.
10. **No exclamation marks in the subject line.** One in the preview text is acceptable but not preferred.

## The Six Styles

Every subject line should be written in one of six styles. When generating options, produce 2–4 styles (not all six) based on what the edition's content supports. Always label which style each option uses.

### Style A: Lead Name + Unexpected Pairing

Pair a recognizable marquee name (event, person, brand, venue) with something surprising or unrelated from the same issue. The familiar name orients the reader. The unexpected item creates a curiosity gap.

**When to use:** Weeks with a clear headliner event or celebrity name plus a quirky secondary item.

**Formula:** [Known Name] + [Unexpected Detail]

**Character target:** 40–48

**Examples:**
- 🎤 Kathleen Madigan Saturday + Shaw Park's Big Reveal
- HarvestFest Returns & Robotic Massages Debut
- 🎹 Elton on the Square + a Ramen Spot Worth the Wait
- Georgia Ballet's Dracula + a German Bakery Opens
- Ina Garten Tonight + a Dumpling Spot We Love
- Polar Bear Run Weekend + a New Poke Spot Opens
- Candlelight Strings + East Cobb's Newest Playground
- Dirty Dancing in Concert + Free Craft Morning
- RV Show at Cobb Galleria + Comedy on the Square
- Valentine's Dinner Sorted + Wheeler Wins Big
- Shakespeare at the Alley + a Sushi Bar on Paper Mill
- Cobb Symphony Saturday + a Pizza Spot Worth Knowing
- MLK Day Events + a Bookstore Opens on the Square
- East Cobb 5K Saturday + a BBQ Joint We've Been Waiting For
- 200 Vendors at Piedmont + Holiday Light Drive
- Gem Show Weekend + East Cobb Lights the Avenue
- Chalk Art Festival + East Cobb's New Axe-Throwing Bar
- Gobble Jog Thanksgiving + Lights of Life Returns
- Special Olympics Kicks Off + a Taco Truck Goes Brick-and-Mortar
- Tree Lighting Friday + East Cobb's Best New Burger

**What makes these work:** The "+" or "&" connects two things that don't obviously belong together, which is what creates the gap. "Tree Lighting" is expected in December. "Best New Burger" is not. That contrast is the engine.

### Style B: Concrete Detail First, Short

Maximum brevity. Two ultra-specific nouns or details joined by a connector. No category words, no verbs, no filler. Every word earns its spot.

**When to use:** Any week. This is the reliable workhorse. Default to this style when unsure.

**Formula:** [Specific Thing] + [Specific Thing]

**Character target:** 30–42

**Examples:**
- Kathleen Madigan + Hand-Pulled Noodles
- 🎄 Tree Lighting + Moxie Burger
- Polar Bear Run + a New Poke Bowl Spot
- Gem Show + Avenue Holiday Lights
- Gobble Jog + Pecan Pie from Scratch
- Chalk Festival + Cumin Beef Noodles
- Ina Garten + Black Inventors Pop-In
- Wheeler Robotics + Shaw Park Plans
- Free Cello Sunday + Saturday Markets
- Robotic Massages + HarvestFest Saturday
- RV Show + Comedy on the Square
- Dirty Dancing Live + Craft Morning
- Dracula Opens + Das Sweet Treat
- Valentine's Dinner + Wheeler Basketball

- Prom Swap + Farmers Market

**What makes these work:** They read like a text message. No setup, no category framing, just two specific things the reader can instantly picture. The shortest path from inbox to "I should open this."

### Style C: Curiosity-First Tease

At least one element creates a gap the reader needs to close by opening. Soft promises ("date night solved"), teases ("the park plan nobody's seen yet"), or specific-but-incomplete details ("a $12 bowl that feeds you twice"). Never clickbait — always delivered in the issue.

**When to use:** Weeks with a strong food or restaurant pick, an unusual event, or a personal recommendation from the editor.

**Formula:** [Concrete Item] + [Tease or Soft Promise]

**Character target:** 38–48

**Examples:**
- Wheeler Robotics to state + date night solved
- Your Saturday morning is already planned
- The park plan nobody's talked about yet
- A lunch spot you'll wish you knew sooner
- East Cobb finally gets a proper BBQ joint
- This weekend's sleeper hit is at a library
- A $12 bowl that feeds you twice
- Two Wheeler teams just punched their ticket
- We found the patio you've been looking for
- Something's happening at Shaw Park
- The restaurant we almost didn't tell you about
- Cobb Energy has a good one Saturday
- You probably drove past this place last week
- Saturday sorted, from morning to late night
- The best East Cobb lunch under $15
- Your next date night just got easier
- A free Saturday morning worth waking up for
- The meeting that'll shape your park for years
- There's a reason this place is always packed
- An East Cobb weekend with zero planning required

**What makes these work:** The reader knows enough to be curious but not enough to skip opening. "A $12 bowl that feeds you twice" — which bowl? Where? The only way to close the gap is to open. But the claim is specific and honest, not bait-and-switch.

**Risk:** Higher than A, B, or D. If overused, readers may start to feel manipulated. Rotate with other styles. Maximum once every 3–4 editions.

### Style D: News-Forward Lead

Lead with the single highest-impact civic, development, or community news item. Signals that ECC/PP is real local journalism, not just an events calendar. Preview text adds the lighter content so the overall package still feels fun.

**When to use:** Weeks with a consequential civic story (zoning, SPLOST, redistricting, school news, infrastructure, public safety).

**Formula:** [Hard News Item] — standalone or with minimal connector

**Character target:** 30–42

**Examples:**
- Shaw Park plans unveiled Thursday
- East Cobb cityhood gets a fresh look
- Wheeler Robotics sends two teams to state
- Marietta payments down after ransomware attack
- SPLOST park plans go public this week
- New traffic signal coming to Johnson Ferry
- Walton rezoning vote set for Tuesday
- Lassiter principal announces retirement
- East Cobb real estate: what actually closed this week
- Cobb PARKS budget gets a $4M boost
- Marietta Square parking changes start Monday
- Fire station 22 breaks ground in East Cobb
- Dickerson MS percussion takes national honors
- New sidewalks approved for Lower Roswell Road
- East Cobb water main project starts next week
- Sprayberry cluster redistricting proposal released
- Cobb library system adds Sunday hours
- Pope High School bond measure on the ballot
- Cobb transit plan public comment closes Friday
- 200+ homes sold in East Cobb last month

**What makes these work:** These subjects say "this newsletter has information you can't get from scrolling Instagram." The preview text then adds entertainment and food hooks so the email still feels warm and fun. The news lead builds credibility; the preview maintains the neighborly brand.

### Style E: Two Punchy Nouns

Absolute minimum word count. Two items, no verbs, no category labels. A telegram. Fast, clear, dense. Emoji optional as a visual anchor.

**When to use:** Weeks with two equally strong items that need no explanation or context.

**Formula:** [Noun/Name] + [Noun/Name]

**Character target:** 25–38

**Examples:**
- 🤖 Wheeler to State + Madigan Saturday
- 🎄 Tree Lighting + Moxie Burger
- 🎃 Boo Bash + Georgia Ballet's Dracula
- Chalk Art + Fall Fun on the Square
- Gobble Jog + Lights of Life
- Polar Bear Run + New East Cobb Eats
- 🎹 Elton Tribute + Artisan Markets
- Gem Show + Avenue Holiday Lights
- 🦃 Turkey Trot + Pecan Pie
- Candlelight Concert + New Thai Spot
- RV Show + Comedy Friday
- Free Museum Day + Matinee Musical
- Prom Swap + Farmers Market
- ShakesBEER + Victorian Secrets

**What makes these work:** Nothing is wasted. The reader processes the entire subject in under a second. Both items are immediately recognizable. Works best when both nouns are genuinely strong — if one is weak, use Style A or B instead to compensate.

### Style F: Conversational / Neighbor Voice

The subject reads like a text from a friend or a line from the editor's note. Casual, first-person, specific. Creates intrigue through personal endorsement rather than headline structure. Biggest departure from standard newsletter convention.

**When to use:** Weeks with a strong personal endorsement (restaurant the editor loved, event the editor attended, something the editor is excited about). Maximum once every 3–4 editions to preserve impact.

**Formula:** [Casual sentence fragment with personal voice]

**Character target:** 35–50

**Examples:**
- The dumpling spot we can't stop thinking about
- We tried the cumin beef noodles. Go.
- Saturday might be our best weekend yet
- There's a free thing at Gritters worth knowing about
- We have opinions about this burger
- This one's a good weekend to leave the house
- Someone tell me how this place is so cheap
- Our Saturday plan, if you want to steal it
- A park meeting you'll actually want to attend
- We went to the Alley Stage and laughed too hard
- Kind of jealous of anyone going Saturday night
- A restaurant so good we drove back the next day
- The thing our kids won't stop talking about
- Found the Valentine's dinner and it's not downtown
- Don't sleep on the farmers market this week
- This might be the best free Saturday in a while
- Okay the pie from this place is unreasonable
- A show at Cobb Energy that's actually affordable
- We're skipping the couch this Saturday
- If you only do one thing this weekend, do this

**What makes these work:** They break the pattern. Every other newsletter in the reader's inbox uses headline-style subjects. This sounds like a human. The first-person voice ("we," "our") is the same neighbor voice readers already know from the blurb, extended into the inbox.

**Risk:** Highest of all six styles. If the voice doesn't land, it can feel too informal or unclear. If overused, the novelty wears off. Reserve for editions where the editorial endorsement is genuinely strong.

## How to Choose a Style

Read the edition content first. Then ask:

1. **Is there a big-name headliner?** → Style A (Lead Name + Unexpected Pairing) or Style E (Two Punchy Nouns)
2. **Is there a major civic/school/infrastructure story?** → Style D (News-Forward)
3. **Is there a restaurant or business the editor personally endorses?** → Style F (Conversational) or Style C (Curiosity Tease)
4. **Are there two equally strong items that need no context?** → Style E (Two Punchy Nouns)
5. **Is it a normal week with solid content but no single standout?** → Style B (Concrete Detail, Short)
6. **Has Style F been used in the last 3 editions?** → Don't use Style F. Pick another.
7. **Has Style C been used in the last 3 editions?** → Don't use Style C. Pick another.

When in doubt, default to Style B. It has the widest range and lowest risk.

## Preview Text Rules

Preview text is the second headline. It must:

1. **Add 2–3 new hooks not in the subject.** Different items, different categories (e.g., if subject covers an event and a restaurant, preview covers news and real estate).
2. **Front-load the strongest remaining hook.** Some clients cut preview text after 40 characters. The first hook must land in that window.
3. **Use connectors:** "Plus," "Also this week," or just commas. Keep it scannable.
4. **Include a count when possible.** "15+ weekend picks" or "3 listings to browse" tells the reader there's volume inside.
5. **Match the energy of the subject.** If the subject is playful (Style F), the preview can be slightly more informational to balance. If the subject is serious (Style D), the preview should add warmth.

**Preview text structure that works:**

```
[Hook 1], [Hook 2], and [Hook 3 or count]
```

**Examples of good pairings:**

| Subject (Style) | Preview Text |
|---|---|
| Kathleen Madigan + Hand-Pulled Noodles (B) | Wheeler Robotics to state, Shaw Park plans Thursday, and 15+ weekend picks |
| Shaw Park plans unveiled Thursday (D) | Plus Kathleen Madigan Saturday, Wheeler Robotics to state, and dumplings on Johnson Ferry |
| The dumpling spot we can't stop thinking about (F) | Kathleen Madigan Saturday, Wheeler Robotics heading to state, and Shaw Park's future gets a first look |
| 🤖 Wheeler to State + Madigan Saturday (E) | Shaw Park plans, a dumpling spot worth the drive, and 15+ things to do this weekend |

## Output Format

When generating subject line options, always deliver in this format:

```
## Subject Line Options

**Option 1 — [Style Name]**
Subject: [subject line] ([character count])
Preview: [preview text]

**Option 2 — [Style Name]**
Subject: [subject line] ([character count])
Preview: [preview text]

**Option 3 — [Style Name]**
Subject: [subject line] ([character count])
Preview: [preview text]
```

Generate 3 options minimum, 4 maximum. Each option must use a different style. Always include the character count for the subject line.

If the user asks for A/B test options, recommend two options that test meaningfully different approaches (e.g., Style B vs. Style D tests entertainment vs. news appeal). Note what each test would tell them about their audience.

## What the Data Says

These patterns come from analysis of 21 ECC editions (Sep 2025 – Feb 2026), with open rates ranging from 44% to 63%:

- **Specificity wins.** The #1 performer (63.17%) named two concrete things: "HarvestFest Returns & Robotic Massages Debut." The worst performers used category words: "Guide," "Tips," "Kickoffs."
- **Familiar + surprising outperforms familiar + familiar.** Pair something the reader expects with something they don't.
- **30–45 characters is the sweet spot.** Under 40 is ideal. Beehiiv's own data confirms under-40 performs best on their platform.
- **Emoji is neutral.** With emoji: 53.0% avg. Without: 54.1% avg. It doesn't hurt but it doesn't help. Use one as a visual anchor when it fits. Skip it when it doesn't.
- **Colons create efficient structure.** "Label: Payoff" packs information into fewer characters. The two subjects that used colons averaged 56.4%.
- **"Guide" and "Tips" in subject lines correlate with lower open rates.** They signal generic content. Avoid.

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Fails | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| "Your Weekend Guide + More" | "Guide" and "More" are invisible filler | Name the actual things in the guide |
| Subject and preview say the same thing | Wastes the only two chances to hook the reader | Preview must add entirely new information |
| Subject over 50 characters | Truncates on mobile, reader sees "..." | Cut to 40. If you can't say it in 40, you haven't found the hook yet |
| Leading with "This Week in East Cobb" | Every local newsletter says this. It's wallpaper | Lead with the most specific, surprising item |
| Two emojis in the subject | Looks spammy, triggers email filters | One max. Zero is fine |
| Using Style F every week | Novelty wears off, starts to feel gimmicky | Max once every 3–4 editions |
| Preview text that starts with "In this issue:" | Turns the preview into a table of contents | Start with a hook, not a label |
| Naming 4+ items in the subject | Reads like a grocery list, nothing stands out | Pick 2. Let the preview handle the rest |
| "Don't Miss" or "You Won't Want to Miss" | Cliché. Every newsletter says it | Be specific about what they'd miss and let the reader decide |

## Inputs Needed Before Writing

If not already provided in context, ask:

1. What are the 3–5 strongest items in this edition? (events, restaurants, news, business)
2. Which item does the editor feel most strongly about?
3. Has Style C or F been used in the last 3 editions?
4. Is there a major civic/news item this week?
5. Are any items time-sensitive (tonight, this Thursday, last chance)?
