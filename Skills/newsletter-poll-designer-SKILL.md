# Newsletter Poll Designer

Designs reader polls that feel like casual community engagement but generate actionable data on which business categories readers are most likely to spend money with. Every poll answer should map to a sponsorable business category.

---

## Why This Skill Exists

Standard newsletter polls ("What's your favorite season?") generate engagement but zero revenue intelligence. The polls this skill creates serve two purposes simultaneously:

1. **Reader-facing:** A fun, relatable question that feels natural in a neighborly local newsletter
2. **Business-facing:** Each answer choice maps directly to a category of local advertiser, so vote totals become a pitch tool ("37% of our readers said they're actively looking for a contractor this spring")

The reader should never feel like they're being surveyed for ad targeting. The framing matters as much as the options.

---

## Platform Context

These newsletters use **Beehiiv's native poll feature**, which means:
- Tap-to-vote (no replies, no A/B/C/D labels)
- Typically 3–5 answer choices (4 is the sweet spot)
- Results are visible to the publisher in Beehiiv's dashboard
- Keep answer text short — these render as tappable buttons in email, not paragraphs

## Step 1: Choose a Framing

The framing is the emotional entry point. It determines how readers interpret the question and how willingly they vote. Different framings surface different business categories.

Here are the proven framings, ranked by versatility:

### Tier 1 — High versatility, works in any edition

**"What's on your to-do list this [season]?"**
- Surfaces: service businesses, appointments, home improvement, camps, restaurants
- Why it works: Feels like commiseration and planning, not a survey. Readers picture their actual life and vote honestly.
- Best for: Spring and fall editions when people are in "get stuff done" mode

**"If someone handed you a $200 gift card to a local business, where's it going?"**
- Surfaces: restaurants, retail, fitness, spas, home improvement, groceries
- Why it works: Aspirational and fun. Readers picture themselves spending, which is the exact purchase-intent signal you want.
- Best for: Any edition. Especially good when you don't have a seasonal hook.

### Tier 2 — Strong but more targeted

**"What's been on your 'I should really handle that' list?"**
- Surfaces: home services (pressure washing, HVAC, gutters), medical/dental, insurance, contractors, auto service
- Why it works: Taps into the nagging-to-do feeling every homeowner has. These are the highest-margin ad categories for local newsletters because these businesses have real customer acquisition costs.
- Best for: Editions with home/seasonal tips already in them (pollen season, winter prep, spring cleaning)

**"What's stressing you out most about your house right now?"**
- Surfaces: lawn care, plumbers, electricians, remodelers, HVAC, pest control, cleaning services
- Why it works: Pain-point driven. Homeowner stress = high purchase intent. Every answer is a service call waiting to happen.
- Best for: Editions that already touch on home maintenance or real estate

**"What's your biggest parenting spend this time of year?"**
- Surfaces: sports leagues, birthday party venues, summer camps, tutoring, childcare, kids' clothing, orthodontists
- Why it works: Parents love comparing notes on costs. Normalizes the question as commiseration, and it maps directly to where family budgets are going.
- Best for: Back-to-school, spring registration season, summer camp season

### Tier 3 — Lifestyle / mood-based

**"What kind of weekend are you craving right now?"**
- Surfaces: coffee shops, restaurants, outdoor gear, date night venues, indoor play spaces, live music venues
- Why it works: Feels like a personality quiz. Low-stakes and fun. Maps to experience-based advertisers.
- Best for: Lighter editions or when you want engagement over sharp targeting

**"If a new business opened walking distance from your house, what would you want it to be?"**
- Surfaces: coffee shops, wine bars, fitness studios, breakfast spots, bakeries, urgent care, pet stores
- Why it works: Wishful thinking framing. Gold for pitching prospective advertisers because you can say "X% of our readers wish they had one of you nearby."
- Best for: Editions covering new business openings or development news

## Step 2: Select 4 Answer Choices

From the chosen framing, pick exactly **4 options**. More than 4 dilutes the vote and makes the poll feel like work. Fewer than 4 doesn't give enough signal.

### Selection criteria (in priority order):

1. **Match the edition's existing content.** If there's a pollen tip in the issue, include a home/HVAC option. If there's a camp-related event, include a summer camp option. The poll should feel like a natural extension of what the reader just consumed.

2. **Prioritize categories you can actually pitch in the next 30 days.** Don't waste a poll slot on a category where you have no advertiser prospects. Pick categories where local businesses exist and are likely to spend on advertising.

3. **Mix high-frequency and high-value.** Include at least one "everyone can relate" option (restaurants, groceries) alongside higher-value service categories (contractors, medical, camps). The relatable option drives total votes; the service options drive ad revenue.

4. **Avoid overlap.** Each option should map to a distinct business category. "Getting the yard in shape" and "Finding a landscaper" are the same slot.

### How each option maps to advertisers:

|---|---|

| Finding a new go-to restaurant | Restaurants, food halls, new openings, brunch spots |
| Booking a family trip | Travel agencies, cabin rentals, family resorts |
| Tackling a home project | Contractors, remodelers, kitchen/bath, interior designers |
| Getting back into fitness | Gyms, yoga/barre studios, personal trainers, running stores |

| A really good coffee shop nearby | Coffee shops, bakeries, café concepts |

## Step 3: Write the Poll

### Format (Beehiiv-ready)

```
## 📊 Reader Poll

**[Question text]**

- [Option 1]
- [Option 2]
- [Option 3]
- [Option 4]
```

### Writing rules:
- **No A/B/C/D labels.** Beehiiv polls are tap-to-vote.
- **No "Reply with..." instructions.** The platform handles voting.
- **Keep option text under 10 words.** These are tappable buttons, not sentences.
- **Use natural, conversational phrasing.** "Getting the yard back in shape" not "Home exterior maintenance services."
- **One emoji in the header is fine** (📊). No emojis on the individual options — they clutter the buttons.
- **Question should be warm and relatable**, not clinical. "What's on your to-do list?" not "Which service category interests you?"

## Step 4: Provide the Advertiser Mapping

After writing the poll, always include a brief mapping note for the user (not for the newsletter). This tells them exactly what each answer means for ad outreach:

```
**Ad intel mapping:**
- Option A → [business categories]
- Option B → [business categories]
- Option C → [business categories]
- Option D → [business categories]
```

## Deployment Strategy

These polls generate the most value when rotated across editions:

- **Run one poll per edition.** Don't double up.
- **Rotate framings every 6–8 weeks.** Use a different Tier 1 or Tier 2 framing each cycle so you build a heat map across many categories over time.
- **Match polls to seasonal content.** Spring → home/yard/camps. Fall → back-to-school/appointments. Winter → home services/holiday spending. Summer → activities/dining/travel.
- **Stack the results across editions.** After 4–6 polls, you have a heat map of reader intent across 20+ business categories. That becomes a media kit slide.
- **Use the language in pitches.** "37% of our readers said they're actively looking for a contractor this spring" is a fundamentally different sales conversation than "we have 4,000 subscribers in the 30062 zip code."

## What Good Looks Like

Here is a poll that was approved for the Feb 26 ECC edition. It matched the seasonal content (pollen tip, spring energy) and mapped to high-value ad categories:

```
## 📊 Reader Poll

**What's on your to-do list this spring?**

- Getting the yard or house back in shape
- Locking down summer camps for the kids
- Scheduling overdue appointments (dentist, doctor, etc.)
- Finding a new go-to restaurant
```

**Why it worked:**
- "Yard or house" tied into the pollen/seasonal tip already in the edition
- "Summer camps" hit the most time-sensitive spending decision East Cobb parents face in spring
- "Overdue appointments" covers high-LTV medical/dental businesses
- "Restaurant" validated the Restaurant Favorite section as a sponsorship vehicle
- Every answer maps to a distinct, pitchable business category

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Fails | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| "What's your favorite season?" | Zero ad intel. Fun but useless for monetization. | Tie every question to spending or needs. |
| 6+ answer options | Dilutes votes, feels like work | Cap at 4. Save extras for future editions. |
| "Which do you prefer: A, B, C, or D?" | Beehiiv is tap-to-vote, no reply needed | Drop the labels and reply instructions. |
| All options map to the same category | Only tests one business vertical | Spread across distinct categories. |
| Options that don't match the edition | Poll feels disconnected from content | Read the edition first, then pick options that extend what's already there. |
| Overly specific options | "Need a plumber" is too narrow to get votes | Keep it category-level: "Something's broken at the house" |
| Generic engagement polls with no ad mapping | Gets clicks but no revenue intelligence | Every option must map to a sponsorable business type. |
